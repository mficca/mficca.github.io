Master’s Thesis: Deep Representation Learning of EHR Sequences

Tools: Python, PyTorch, ConvAE, UMAP, pandas

Data: MIMIC-III (sample subset provided)

Description:
Developed a convolutional autoencoder (ConvAE) to learn latent patient representations from longitudinal EHR data, enabling unsupervised clustering into clinically meaningful subgroups.

  • Objective: Extract robust vector embeddings for patient stratification.
  • Method: CNN-based autoencoder trained on fixed-length subsequences of medical concept codes.
  • Evaluation: Token-level reconstruction accuracy (≈45%), UMAP visualization, cluster demographics analysis.
  • Key Findings: Identified three clusters with distinct age, gender, and mortality profiles.

Cluster Findings

Cluster 0

Cluster 0 contains patients predominantly in middle-age and early senior years (50–60: 19.1%, 60–70: 17.9%, 70–80: 13.9%). The group has a balanced gender distribution (54% male, 46% female) and moderate mortality rates (9.3% hospital deaths, 29.0% overall deaths). Very few patients are under age 20, indicating this cluster captures older, chronically treated individuals.

Cluster 1

Cluster 1 skews slightly older, with peaks in the 60–80 age range (60–70: 22.6%, 70–80: 25.8%). It has the highest male predominance (61.7%) and lower hospital mortality (5.2%), but a similar overall death rate (31.0%). This suggests a cohort with significant comorbidities but better acute survival.

Cluster 2

Cluster 2 exhibits the highest mortality (29.5% hospital deaths, 57.6% overall deaths) and a mixed age profile (60–70: 19.3%, 70–80: 20.7%, 80–90: 17.1%, 90+: 6.6%). The gender split is near even (54.8% male, 45.2% female). This cluster likely represents the most critically ill and frail patients in the study.

Cluster Interpretations

Across the three ConvAE–derived patient clusters, clear differences emerge in age profiles, gender balance, and mortality outcomes, suggesting distinct clinical phenotypes:

  1. Age and Gender Patterns
    Cluster 0 skews toward middle-aged adults (50–70), with balanced gender representation (54% male). Its low proportion of very elderly patients (90+ years: 3.6%) and minimal youth involvement (<1% under age 10) point to a cohort of chronically managed, community-dwelling individuals.
    Cluster 1 captures a slightly older population (peak 60–80 years) with stronger male predominance (62%). The steep rise in the 70–80 band (26%) suggests patients with age-related but relatively stable comorbidities.
    Cluster 2 shows the broadest age distribution (higher shares in 80–90 and 90+ bands) and a near-even gender split, marking it as the frailest cohort.
  2. Mortality Outcomes
    Cluster 0 shows moderate in-hospital (9.3%) and overall (29.0%) mortality, consistent with its younger, healthier composition.
    Cluster 1, despite older age, has lower hospital mortality (5.2%), implying better acute resilience or more elective care pathways, even though its overall mortality remains elevated (31.0%).
    Cluster 2 exhibits the highest hospital (29.5%) and overall (57.6%) death rates, underscoring its identification of high-risk, severely ill patients.
  3. Clinical Implications
    These clusters could guide tailored care strategies: Cluster 0 may benefit from chronic disease management; Cluster 1 from preventive interventions to sustain acute stability; and Cluster 2 from palliative or intensive support resources.
